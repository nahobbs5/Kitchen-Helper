
## A

**À la carte** — Ordering individual dishes separately, each priced independently.
**À la king** — Dish served in a cream sauce with peppers and mushrooms.
**À la minute** — Cooked to order at the last moment, ensuring freshness.
**À la mode** — Served with ice cream (US); or in a particular style (French).
**Aerate** — To incorporate air into a mixture, often by sifting or whisking.
**Aglio e olio** — Italian sauce of garlic and olive oil.
**Agnolotti** — Small stuffed pasta from Piedmont, Italy.
**Al dente** — Italian for 'to the tooth'; pasta or vegetables cooked until just firm.
**Al forno** — Italian for 'from the oven'; baked or roasted.
**Aioli** — Garlic-flavored mayonnaise originating from Provence.
**Albumin** — Protein in egg whites that coagulates when heated.
**Allumette** — Vegetables or potatoes cut into thin matchstick strips.
**Amandine** — Garnished or prepared with almonds.
**Amuse-bouche** — A single-bite hors d'oeuvre served before a meal.
**Andouille** — Smoked pork sausage used in Cajun and French cooking.
**Anglaise** — Basic custard sauce made with eggs, sugar, and milk.
**Antipasto** — Italian appetizer course served before the main meal.
**Appareil** — A prepared mixture used as a base in cooking.
**Arborio** — Short-grain Italian rice used to make risotto.
**Aromatize** — To add aromatic flavors via herbs, spices, or other ingredients.
**Aspic** — A savory jelly made from clarified meat or fish stock.
**Au beurre** — Cooked or finished with butter.
**Au gratin** — Topped with breadcrumbs or cheese and browned under a broiler.
**Au jus** — Served with its own natural cooking juices.
**Au lait** — Prepared or served with milk.
**Au naturel** — Cooked simply, without elaborate preparation or sauces.
**Au poivre** — Prepared with cracked black pepper.
**Au sec** — Reduced until almost dry during cooking.
**Avgolemono** — Greek soup or sauce made with eggs and lemon juice.

## B

**Bain-marie** — A water bath used to gently heat or keep food warm.
**Bake** — To cook food using dry heat in an oven.
**Bake blind** — To pre-bake a pastry shell without filling, using weights.
**Ballotine** — Meat or poultry that has been boned, stuffed, and rolled.
**Bard** — To wrap lean meat in fat before roasting to prevent drying.
**Baste** — To spoon or brush pan juices over meat during cooking.
**Baton** — Vegetables cut into thick rectangular sticks, larger than julienne.
**Batter** — A pourable mixture of flour, liquid, and often eggs.
**Beard** — To remove the thread-like fibrous part from mussels.
**Beat** — To mix rapidly with a spoon, whisk, or mixer to add air.
**Béchamel** — Classic French white sauce made from butter, flour, and milk.
**Beignet** — Deep-fried fritter, often made with choux pastry dough.
**Beurre blanc** — Emulsified butter sauce made with white wine and shallots.
**Beurre manié** — Equal parts butter and flour kneaded together to thicken sauces.
**Beurre noir** — Butter cooked until dark brown, often with capers and vinegar.
**Beurre noisette** — Butter cooked until golden brown and nutty in aroma.
**Bisque** — A smooth, creamy shellfish soup.
**Blanch** — To plunge food briefly in boiling water then into ice water.
**Blend** — To mix ingredients together until smooth or uniformly combined.
**Bloom** — To soften gelatin in cold liquid before dissolving; or activate yeast.
**Boil** — To cook in liquid at 212°F (100°C), with vigorous bubbling.
**Boning** — Removing bones from meat, poultry, or fish.
**Bouillon** — A clear, thin broth made from simmered meat or vegetables.
**Bouquet garni** — Bundle of herbs tied together and used to flavor stocks and stews.
**Braise** — To cook slowly in a small amount of liquid in a covered pot.
**Brine** — A salt-water solution used to preserve or season food.
**Broach** — To spit-roast meat over an open fire.
**Broil** — To cook food directly under a high heat source (UK: grill).
**Brown** — To cook until surface turns brown via Maillard reaction.
**Brunoise** — Vegetables diced very finely into tiny uniform cubes.
**Butterfly** — To split food almost in two and open it flat.

## C

**Caramelize** — To heat sugar until it melts and turns golden brown.
**Carve** — To slice cooked meat or poultry for serving.
**Casserole** — A slow-cooked dish made in an oven-proof pot; also the pot itself.
**Chiffonade** — Leafy vegetables or herbs cut into very thin ribbons.
**Chop** — To cut food into irregular pieces with a knife.
**Clarify** — To make a liquid clear by removing impurities; also to melt butter.
**Coat** — To cover food with a layer of flour, egg, breadcrumbs, or sauce.
**Coddle** — To cook eggs gently in water just below the boiling point.
**Colander** — A perforated bowl used to drain pasta, vegetables, or liquids.
**Compote** — Fresh or dried fruit cooked in sugar syrup.
**Concassé** — Peeled, seeded, and roughly chopped tomatoes.
**Confit** — Meat slow-cooked in its own fat; also fruit preserved in sugar.
**Consommé** — A perfectly clear, rich, strongly flavored stock.
**Coulis** — A smooth, pourable sauce made from puréed fruit or vegetables.
**Court-bouillon** — Flavored liquid used for poaching fish or shellfish.
**Cream** — To beat fat and sugar together until light and fluffy.
**Crimp** — To seal and decorate the edges of pastry.
**Crouton** — Small cubes of fried or toasted bread used as a garnish.
**Crudités** — Raw vegetables served with a dipping sauce as an appetizer.
**Cure** — To preserve food using salt, sugar, smoke, or acid.
**Cut in** — To work cold fat into flour using a pastry cutter or two knives.

## D

**Dash** — A very small amount of liquid, roughly ⅛ teaspoon.
**Daube** — A French braised meat stew, typically beef or lamb.
**Debeard** — To remove the beard (byssal threads) from mussels.
**Deglaze** — To add liquid to a hot pan to release browned bits.
**Degrease** — To remove fat from the surface of a stock, sauce, or broth.
**Dégorger** — To draw out moisture or bitterness from vegetables by salting.
**Demi-glace** — A rich, gelatinous sauce made by reducing brown stock.
**Denaturation** — When proteins unfold and change structure due to heat or acid.
**Devein** — To remove the dark digestive tract from shrimp.
**Dice** — To cut food into small, uniform cubes.
**Dredge** — To coat food lightly and evenly with flour or breadcrumbs.
**Dress** — To prepare a salad with dressing; or to clean poultry.
**Drizzle** — To pour a thin stream of liquid over food.
**Dry rub** — A mixture of dry spices applied to meat before cooking.
**Dust** — To sprinkle lightly with a powdered ingredient like flour or sugar.

## E

**Emulsify** — To combine two unmixable liquids into a stable mixture.
**En croûte** — Food enclosed in a pastry crust before baking.
**En papillote** — Food cooked in a sealed parcel of parchment or foil.
**Escabeche** — A dish of fried fish marinated in a spiced vinegar sauce.
**Espagnole** — One of the five classic French mother sauces; a rich brown sauce.
**Essence** — A concentrated extract used to flavor food.
**Etuvée** — Slow, covered cooking method using minimal liquid.
**Evaporate** — To remove liquid from a dish by gentle heating.
**Extrude** — To force dough or paste through a shaped die to create pasta.

## F

**Farce** — Forcemeat or stuffing used to fill poultry, fish, or vegetables.
**Ferment** — To allow bacteria or yeast to transform sugars, developing flavor.
**Filet** — A boneless cut of meat or fish.
**Fines herbes** — A mixture of chopped fresh herbs: parsley, chives, tarragon, chervil.
**Flambé** — To ignite alcohol in a dish, burning off the alcohol.
**Fold** — To gently incorporate ingredients using a cutting and turning motion.
**Fond** — Browned bits left on a pan bottom after sautéing; used for sauces.
**Fondue** — A dish where food is dipped into a communal pot of melted cheese or broth.
**Forcemeat** — A ground, emulsified meat mixture used for sausages and terrines.
**French** — To trim meat from the bone end of a rib chop or rack.
**Fricassee** — A white stew of sautéed meat in a cream sauce.
**Frittata** — Italian open-faced omelette cooked on the stovetop and broiled.
**Fry** — To cook food in hot oil or fat.
**Fumet** — A concentrated fish stock used as a base for sauces.

## G

**Galette** — A flat, free-form pastry; also a savory buckwheat crêpe.
**Ganache** — A smooth emulsion of cream and melted chocolate.
**Garnish** — A decorative addition to a dish, often edible.
**Gastrique** — Caramelized sugar deglazed with vinegar for sweet-savory sauces.
**Gel** — To set a liquid into a solid or semi-solid using gelatin or starch.
**Gelatin** — A protein derived from animal collagen that sets liquids when cooled.
**Glaze** — A shiny coating applied to food; or to cook until a coating forms.
**Grate** — To reduce food to shreds or fine particles using a grater.
**Gratinée** — Browned and crisped under a broiler or with a torch.
**Grease** — To coat a pan with fat to prevent sticking.
**Gremolata** — A condiment of lemon zest, garlic, and parsley.
**Grind** — To crush food into a powder or coarse meal.
**Gristle** — Tough cartilage or connective tissue in meat.
**Gut** — To clean and remove the internal organs from fish or poultry.

## H

**Haute cuisine** — The style of elaborate, refined French restaurant cooking.
**Hollandaise** — Classic French emulsified butter sauce with egg yolks and lemon.
**Hors d'oeuvre** — Small appetizers served before a meal.
**Hull** — To remove the outer shell or husk from grains, seeds, or berries.
**Hydrate** — To add water or liquid to a dry ingredient to reconstitute it.
**Hydrolysis** — Breaking down proteins or starches through water and heat.

## I

**Icing** — A sweet coating applied to baked goods; also called frosting.
**Infuse** — To steep ingredients in hot liquid to extract flavor.
**Italian meringue** — Meringue made by beating hot sugar syrup into egg whites.

## J

**Jus** — The natural juices released by meat during cooking.
**Julienne** — Vegetables cut into thin, uniform matchstick strips.

## K

**Knead** — To work dough by folding and pressing to develop gluten.
**Knock back** — To punch down risen dough to expel gas before shaping.

## L

**Lard** — Rendered pig fat used as cooking fat; also to insert fat into meat.
**Lardons** — Small cubes or strips of bacon or pork fat, often used in stews.
**Leavening** — An agent that causes dough or batter to rise (yeast, baking powder).
**Liaison** — A thickening agent added to sauces, often egg yolks and cream.
**Lightly floured** — Dusted with just enough flour to prevent sticking.
**Line** — To cover the inside of a pan with pastry or parchment paper.
**Lukewarm** — Slightly warm; approximately 95–105°F (35–40°C).

## M

**Macerate** — To soak fruit in liquid (often alcohol) to soften and flavor it.
**Maillard reaction** — The chemical browning of proteins and sugars under dry heat.
**Mandoline** — A slicing tool with adjustable blades for thin, uniform cuts.
**Marinate** — To soak food in a seasoned liquid to add flavor and tenderness.
**Mask** — To coat food completely with sauce or aspic.
**Mince** — To cut food into very fine pieces.
**Mise en place** — French for 'everything in its place'; preparation before cooking.
**Mirepoix** — A combination of diced onion, carrot, and celery used as a flavor base.
**Monter au beurre** — To finish a sauce by whisking in cold butter for richness.
**Mousse** — A light, airy dish made with whipped cream or egg whites.
**Mull** — To heat a beverage like wine or cider with spices.

## N

**Nap** — To lightly coat food with sauce.
**Nappe** — The consistency of a sauce thick enough to coat a spoon.
**Noisette** — Small round cuts of meat; also hazelnut butter (beurre noisette).
**Nougat** — A confection made from cooked sugar, nuts, and egg whites.

## O

**Oblique cut** — Diagonal cut used on cylindrical vegetables for stir-fries.
**Offal** — Internal organs and entrails of an animal used as food.
**Oven-proof** — Suitable for use in the oven at cooking temperatures.
**Overcook** — To cook beyond the point where food is optimally done.

## P

**Panade** — A paste of bread, flour, or rice used to bind forcemeats.
**Parboil** — To partially cook food by boiling before finishing another way.
**Pare** — To peel or trim the outer skin from vegetables or fruit.
**Pâte** — French for dough or pastry; also used to describe pâté.
**Pâté** — Seasoned ground meat and fat mixture cooked in a mold.
**Paupiette** — Thin slice of meat rolled around a stuffing.
**Pearling** — Removing the outer bran layer of grains through abrasion.
**Persillade** — A mixture of parsley and garlic used as a seasoning or garnish.
**Pickle** — To preserve food in brine or vinegar solution.
**Pilaf** — Method of cooking rice by first sautéing then simmering in stock.
**Pinch** — The amount of a dry ingredient held between two fingers; about 1/16 tsp.
**Pit** — To remove the stone or pit from fruit.
**Poach** — To cook gently in barely simmering liquid.
**Purée** — To process food until completely smooth using a blender or food mill.

## Q

**Quenelle** — An elegant oval shape formed from soft mixture using two spoons.
**Quick bread** — Bread leavened with baking powder or soda instead of yeast.

## R

**Ragu** — A meat-based Italian sauce slow-cooked with tomatoes.
**Ramekin** — A small individual baking dish.
**Ratatouille** — Provençal vegetable stew of eggplant, zucchini, peppers, and tomatoes.
**Reduce** — To boil a liquid to concentrate flavor and thicken consistency.
**Refresh** — To rinse cooked vegetables in cold water to stop cooking.
**Render** — To slowly heat fatty meat to melt and release the fat.
**Rest** — To let cooked meat sit before cutting, allowing juices to redistribute.
**Roast** — To cook uncovered in an oven using dry heat.
**Rondelle** — Round slices cut from cylindrical vegetables.
**Roux** — A cooked mixture of equal parts fat and flour used to thicken sauces.
**Rub** — A dry mixture of spices and herbs applied to meat before cooking.

## S

**Sabayon** — A light sauce made by whisking egg yolks with liquid over heat.
**Salt cure** — Preserving food by packing in salt to draw out moisture.
**Sauce** — A liquid or semi-liquid dressing or accompaniment for food.
**Sauté** — To cook quickly in a small amount of fat over high heat.
**Scald** — To heat milk just below boiling; also to blanch briefly.
**Score** — To make shallow cuts in food to aid cooking or presentation.
**Sear** — To brown the surface of meat rapidly over high heat.
**Season** — To enhance flavor with salt, pepper, herbs, and spices.
**Shred** — To cut or tear food into long, thin strips.
**Shuck** — To remove the shell from oysters, clams, or corn.
**Sieve** — To press food through a fine-mesh strainer to smooth it.
**Sift** — To pass dry ingredients through a fine sieve to aerate and remove lumps.
**Simmer** — To cook in liquid just below boiling, with gentle bubbling.
**Skim** — To remove fat or foam from the surface of a liquid.
**Smoke** — To flavor food by exposing it to wood smoke.
**Sous vide** — Cooking vacuum-sealed food in a temperature-controlled water bath.
**Steam** — To cook food over boiling water using steam.
**Steep** — To soak ingredients in hot liquid to extract flavor.
**Stew** — To cook food slowly in a liquid, fully submerged.
**Stir-fry** — To cook quickly in a small amount of oil over very high heat.
**Strain** — To pass liquid through a sieve or cheesecloth to remove solids.
**Stuff** — To fill a cavity in meat, poultry, or vegetables with a filling.
**Supreme** — The boneless breast of poultry; also a segment of citrus.
**Sweat** — To cook aromatics gently in fat without browning.

## T

**Temper** — To gradually add hot liquid to eggs to raise their temperature safely.
**Tenderize** — To break down tough meat fibers through mechanical or chemical means.
**Terrine** — A loaf-shaped mold; also the dish cooked in one.
**Toast** — To brown the surface of bread, nuts, or spices with dry heat.
**Torte** — A rich, dense cake, often made with little or no flour.
**Toss** — To mix ingredients by lifting and turning them together.
**Tournée** — A vegetable cut into a seven-sided football shape.
**Truss** — To tie poultry or meat with string to maintain shape during cooking.
**Turn** — To cut vegetables into uniform oval shapes; or to flip food while cooking.

## U

**Umami** — The fifth basic taste; a savory flavor found in aged cheese, meat, and soy.
**Unmold** — To remove food from a mold after it has set or baked.

## V

**Vandyke** — To cut a decorative zigzag border on fruit or vegetables.
**Velouté** — A classic French mother sauce made from light stock and roux.
**Vinaigrette** — An emulsion of oil and vinegar used as a salad dressing.
**Vol-au-vent** — A hollow puff pastry case filled with savory ingredients.

## W

**Whip** — To beat rapidly to incorporate air and increase volume.
**Whisk** — To beat rapidly with a wire whisk; also the tool itself.
**Wilt** — To soften greens by applying gentle heat.
**Work** — To knead, mix, or fold a mixture repeatedly.

## X

**Xanthan gum** — A food additive used as a thickener and stabilizer in cooking.
**Xiaolongbao** — Delicate Chinese soup dumplings filled with broth and meat.

## Y

**Yeast** — A leavening agent that ferments sugars to produce CO₂ and alcohol.
**Yield** — The amount of usable product after preparation or cooking.

## Z

**Zest** — The outer colored layer of citrus peel, used for flavor.
**Zester** — A tool used to remove zest from citrus fruit.
**Zucchini** — Versatile summer squash; used both raw and cooked in many cuisines.
